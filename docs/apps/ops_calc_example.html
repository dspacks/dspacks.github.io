Foundational Rules of Operations Management

This section covers core principles; these rules apply to all manufacturing and service settings.

Capacity Cushion

Operations managers maintain a safety buffer; this is often a percentage of total time. This buffer helps manage unexpected demand; it covers machine maintenance and worker absences.

Bottleneck Identification

The bottleneck is the step with the longest processing time; it has the lowest output rate per hour. This single step limits the output of the entire system; improving other steps does not increase total output.

Long-Term vs. Short-Term Decisions

Long-term decisions involve buying buildings or large machines; short-term decisions focus on finding and resolving immediate work blockages.

Line Balancing

Balancing an assembly line involves matching work step times to a target rate; this matches production to customer demand; it minimizes idle time.

Step-by-Step Derivation

1. Calculate Client X Workload Time:

Total = (D_x * p_x) + ((D_x / Q_x) * s_x)

2. Calculate Client Y Workload Time:

Total = (D_y * p_y) + ((D_y / Q_y) * s_y)

3. Calculate Effective Available Time per Machine:

Available = N * (1 - Cushion)

4. Solve for Number of Copiers (M):

M = Total Hours / Available Hours

MBA Discussion Point: Decimals and Overtime

The division results in a fraction of a machine. Because the business cannot buy a fraction of a machine; it must round up. If the fraction is very small; managers may choose to pay overtime to current staff or shorten the cushion instead of purchasing an expensive piece of equipment.

Mathematical Steps Used

1. Cycle Time (C):

C = Available Time / Demand

2. Theoretical Minimum Stations (TM):

TM = Total Task Time / Cycle Time

3. Process Efficiency (E):

E = Total Task Time / (Actual Stations * Cycle Time)
